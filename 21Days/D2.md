## Frameworks (voluntary)

### (Most Critical) NIST CSF (National Institute of Standards and Technology Cybersecurity Framework) 
> Universal language for talking about cybersecurity risk
> 6 functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover.
- Govern → Who's in charge and what are the rules?
- Identify → What assets do we have and what are the risks?
- Protect → What controls are in place to prevent harm?
- Detect → How do we know when something goes wrong?
- Respond → What do we do when an incident happens?
- Recover → How do we get back to normal?

### NIST 800-53 (Federal baseline)
> Massive catalog of ~1,000 security controls organized into 20 control families — originally built for federal agencies, now used as a reference by everyone.
> CSF tells you what category of security activity to perform. 
> 800-53 tells you exactly which control satisfies it.

Family | Code | What it covers | 
-------|------|----------------|
Access Control | AC | Who can get in |
Audit & Accountability | AU | Logging everything |
Incident Response | IR | When things break |
Risk Assessment | RA | Finding threats |
System & Comms Protection | SC | Encryption, network |

*Tailored Basline*
> Nobody implements all 1,000 controls. The real skill is scoping — knowing which controls apply to your environment, your threat model, your regulatory requirement.
 
### ISO 27001 (International Certifiable standard)
> Promotes building an Information Security Management System (ISMS)
> The processes, policies, people, and continuous improvement loop are themselves the product.
> ISO 27001 has an "Annex A" — 93 controls across 4 themes. Think of it as ISO's version of 800-53 but much leaner.
> Unlike NIST, Certifications are available for ISO 27001 that can be audited by third party auditors. 
> ISO 27001 certified means an external auditor verified their security program meets the standard. That's a trust signal to clients, partners, and regulators.

### SOC 2 (Service Organization Controls 2) or (SaaS Trust Report)
> Auditor-issued report that proves a service organization handles customer data securely across 5 Trust Service Criteria.
> Based on AICPA (American Institute of CPAs)
> 5 Trust Services Criteria — security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, privacy

- Security - Protected against unauthorized access (only mandatory one)  
- Availability - System is up when promised  
- Processing Integrity - Data processed correctly  
- Confidentiality -  Sensitive data protected  
- Privacy -  PII handled properly 

> Not a certification, but an *attestation* → audited by external CPA firms every 1-3 years. Two levels:
- SOC 2 Type 1 → auditor reviewed controls at a single point in time.   
- SOC 2 Type 2 → auditor reviewed controls over a period (6-12 months).

### PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)
> Mandatory if you touch card data. Not a framework, it's a *contract* you sign with Visa/MC/Amex when you want to process payments.
> Visa, Mastercard, Amex, Discover enforces through merchant agreements. Violate it → fines, increased transaction fees, or losing the ability to accept cards entirely.
> 12 requirements across 6 domains. All mandatory. No "scoping" — if you accept cards, you do all of it.  
Groups: 
| Goal | Focus | 
|------|-------| 
| Build & maintain secure network | Firewalls, no vendor defaults | 
| Protect cardholder data | Encryption, storage limits | 
| Vulnerability management | Patching, antivirus | 
| Access control | Need-to-know, unique IDs | 
| Monitor & test | Logging, penetration testing | 
| Information security policy | Written program | 

Requirements:
| Requirement | What it covers  | 
|---------------|----------------| 
| 1-2 | Build and maintain a secure network | 
| 3 | Protect stored cardholder data | 
| 4 | Encrypt transmission of cardholder data | 
| 5 | Protect all systems against malware | 
| 6 | Develop and maintain secure systems and software | 
| 7 | Restrict access to cardholder data | 
| 8 | Identify and authenticate access to system components | 
| 9 | Restrict physical access to cardholder data | 
| 10 | Log and monitor all access to cardholder data and network resources | 
| 11 | Regularly test security systems and processes | 
| 12 | Maintain a policy that addresses information security for all personnel |

**PCI DSS is live in their environment right now — which makes it a natural artifact reference even though your portfolio targets FTC Safeguards.**

## Regulations (mandatory by Law)

### FTC Safeguards Rule - Issued under the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA). Originally for banks
> Expanded in 2023 to cover auto dealerships, mortgage brokers, tax preparers, anyone handling consumer financial data.
> Legally required to have a written information security program. No option to opt out. (Revisiting Day 1 of the fundementals of GRC)
> The 9 required elements define what a legally compliant written information security program must contain — covered in full on Day 3.

### HIPAA
> Protects Protected Health Information (PHI). Covers healthcare providers, insurers, and their vendors (called Business Associates).
> Two rules: Privacy Rule (who can see data) and Security Rule (how to protect it technically).   

### GDPR / CCPA
| GDPR | CCPA |
|------|------|
| Jurisdiction | EU residents | California residents |
| Core right | Right to erasure | Right to opt out of data sale |
| Enforcer | EU Data Protection Authorities | California AG |

## The universal rule across ALL regulations:


**Regulations tell you what outcome is required. Frameworks tell you how to achieve it.**
