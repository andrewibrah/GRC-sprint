# Policy Hierarchy
> **Policy** — A formal declaration of intent by an authority. It exists to create accountability. If there's no policy, no one is officially responsible for anything.
> **Standard** — A baseline. The minimum acceptable state. Below this line = non-compliant. It's a measurement tool.
> **Procedure** — A sequence of actions that must be followed to achieve a consistent outcome. Removes human interpretation from the equation.
> **Guideline** — An informed recommendation. No enforcement mechanism. Exists to share expertise, not mandate behavior.

# Authority vs Specificity
Policy has the most authority and the least specificity. Guidelines have the least authority and can be highly specific.


# The 3 GRC Pillars
> **Governance** - Who has the authority to make decisions, and are they being held accountable for outcomes?
The power structure of an organization is made explicit and documented.
> **Risk** - Formal discipline of identifying what could go wrong, how likely it is, and how bad it would be.
Deciding what to do about it turns uncertainty into a manageable variable.
> **Compliance** - Proof that you did what you were legally or contractually obligated to do. 
Not security. Not safety. Proof of obligation met.

**Governance sets the rules. Risk identifies the threats to those rules. Compliance proves the rules are being followed.** 

# D1 - Delverable 
In today's technological world, Accuracy, Assurance, and Accountability are the most essential when it comes to Software. This is where companies use processes such as policies, risk assessments, written procedures, and guidelines to uphold standards for assigning responsibility for decisions, identifying and managing risks, and documenting compliance. You may know these as the Policy Hierarchy and the three GRC Pillars.

Policy Hierarchy sorts by authority from highest to lowest and specificity from lowest to highest. Policies are formally written statements of intent for an organization. Creating accountability for decision makers against the highest authority. Organizations create standards to create a baseline to compare where we are currently. Organizations write procedures to provide consistent steps to achieve a consistent outcome, while guidelines are simply informed recommendations that are not enforceable but have been shown to be effective in specific situations.

When talking about the 3 GRC pillars, it is important to remind ourselves that this is a system of management. Governance ensures someone is responsible to make decisions and take accountability for the outcomes of the team. The pillar of risk is mastered by understand how likely one may be taken advantage of. Investigating the vulnerabilities and inefficiencies of a system is the most critical skill in todays age, it is very important to have this skill on your side in order to deeply understand threats and patchs. Lastly, compliance is simply showing your work for an audit. If your company cannot prove they paid taxes, who will believe them? If they can't show logs of users details when accessing data, who can trust them? If they can't prove they have a written agreement of who can make decisions, who can be held accountable?

You see, if we don't have Governance on decision making regarding an organizations policy hierarchy, we create chaos. With that Chaos comes high risk, without a risk officer that chaos turns into customer and attacker threats. Soon enough, compliance regulators come knocking on the door for evidence you've been doing the right things, but all you have is a broken system with empty records, 0 reports and 0 Organizational assets of any kind.

The power of a GRC officer is to bring order to this chaos. Checking and ensuring updates to Organizational Policies & Resources, working with security teams to frequently assess and patch potential risks, and lastly, ensure a system or process is set in place for tracking, comparing and reporting compliance and regulatory requirements against standards and regulation. GRC officers do this by ensuring evidence of commitment and accountability for decisions.
